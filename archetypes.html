Personality Profiles
A
The Ally
Gold · #D4A843
Amplifies others. Builds bridges between teams. Creates psychological safety where honest conversations happen. The leader people seek when they need someone to listen without judgment.
Strengths
  • Exceptional listener who absorbs context before responding
  • Bridge-builder across teams, functions, and seniority levels
  • Creates safe spaces for honest feedback and vulnerable conversations
Watch-outs
  • Can over-accommodate at the expense of their own needs or opinion
  • May avoid necessary conflict, letting tensions fester
“My job isn’t to have the answers. It’s to make sure every voice in the room gets heard.”
A
The Architect
Teal · #0D9488
Sees the system when others see chaos. Designs org structures, design systems, and processes that scale. Thinks in dependencies and leverage points.
Strengths
  • Systems thinker who maps complexity into clear models
  • Pattern recognition across seemingly unrelated domains
  • Scales impact through structure, process, and tooling
Watch-outs
  • Can over-systematize, creating rigidity where flexibility is needed
  • May prioritize structure over people and relationships
“Great design organizations aren’t built by accident. They’re architected.”
P
The Pathfinder
Blue · #2563EB
Charts new territory. Questions every assumption. Leads by exploring what nobody else has tried. Most comfortable at the frontier where the map runs out.
Strengths
  • Visionary thinking that reframes problems entirely
  • Challenges the status quo with courage and conviction
  • Inspires teams through exploration and bold experiments
Watch-outs
  • Can chase novelty over stability, leaving proven approaches behind
  • May move so fast that teams struggle to keep up
“The best answer is usually hiding behind the question nobody thought to ask.”
A
The Anchor
Coral · #E8927C
Holds the center when everything else moves. Absorbs uncertainty. Provides calm clarity in chaos. The leader teams rely on during crisis and high-stakes moments.
Strengths
  • Emotional steadiness that calms entire teams under pressure
  • Provides clarity and direction when ambiguity is highest
  • Deep domain expertise built through years of focused practice
Watch-outs
  • Can resist necessary change, holding on to what worked before
  • May absorb too much stress personally instead of distributing it
“My team doesn’t need me to panic with them. They need me to be the calm.”
C
The Cultivator
Olive · #65783E
Grows teams. Sees raw talent and nurtures it. Builds culture as competitive advantage. Measures success not by what they ship, but by who they help grow.
Strengths
  • Talent development that turns juniors into future leaders
  • Culture building that attracts and retains top designers
  • Long-term team growth measured in careers, not sprints
Watch-outs
  • Can over-invest in struggling performers at the team’s expense
  • May neglect their own growth while focused on others
“The best design team I ever led wasn’t the most talented. It was the most nurtured.”

Usage Guidelines
Archetypes appear across both brands in specific, defined contexts. They are additive to the expression layer — they never replace it.
Reveal Moment
After completing the leadership assessment, users see their archetype in a full-screen reveal. Instrument Serif italic name, archetype color radial gradient background, film grain at 15%. The single most dramatic use of archetype color in the system.
Profile Badge
A small pill-shaped badge on user profiles showing their archetype. Archetype color border with transparent fill. Appears on platform profiles, community cards, and speaker bios. Always compact — never dominates the layout.
Share Cards
Social-ready cards for sharing archetype results on LinkedIn, Twitter, and other platforms. Solid archetype color background with avatar, name, archetype title, and the community URL. Optimized for 1200×630 OG image dimensions.
Conference Lanyards
Physical badges at DLS with archetype color on the top half (name in Instrument Serif italic, film grain) and Bauhaus block composition on the cream bottom half. The one place where archetype and DLS expression merge on a single artifact.
Content Personalization
Articles, resources, and tool recommendations tagged by archetype relevance. An Architect sees systems-thinking content surfaced first. A Cultivator sees team-building resources. Subtle archetype color accent on recommendation cards.
Community Grouping
Slack channels, meetup groups, and cohort sessions organized by archetype. Peer groups for shared challenges. Archetype color used as channel icon accent and group badge. Cross-archetype events encouraged for diversity of thought.
Visual Rules
Archetype colors are additive to the expression color system. They layer on top — they never substitute.
Do
Use archetype colors for archetype-specific surfaces: reveal cards, share cards, profile badges, lanyard tops, and personalization accents.
Don’t
Replace expression colors (DLS purple, Salary green, Jobs blue, etc.) with archetype colors. A Salary page stays neon green even if the user is an Architect.
Do
Use radial-gradient from primary to dark variant for archetype gradient surfaces. Film grain at 15% on dark archetype backgrounds.
Don’t
Use linear gradients for archetype colors. All archetype gradients are radial, emanating from center — this distinguishes them from expression gradients.
Do
Set archetype names in Instrument Serif italic on reveal cards and lanyards. This is the typographic signature of the archetype system.
Don’t
Use Instrument Serif for archetype names in UI elements like badges, tags, or navigation. Those stay in DM Sans to match the platform typography.
Do
Make archetype badges pill-shaped with a 2px archetype color border and transparent fill. Keep them small and subordinate to the main content.
Don’t
Create large archetype-colored UI sections or backgrounds in the platform. Outside of reveal moments, archetype color should be an accent, not a surface.
