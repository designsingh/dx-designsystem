DESIGNX + DLS
BRAND GUIDE RESTRUCTURE · FEB 2026
Section 01 — The Problem

The Reframe

A brand identity guide exists to answer three questions. The current site tries to answer those questions but also tries to be an engineering spec, a product walkthrough, and a content strategy deck. It needs to pick a lane.

Three Questions a Brand Guide Answers
01
Identity
Who are we?
Brand positioning, voice, personality, values. The story that every other decision hangs from.
02
Visual Language
What do we look like?
Color, type, texture, imagery, layout. The visual toolkit expressed as principles, not code.
03
Application
How do we apply it?
Patterns for real surfaces: cards, pages, social, print. Practical guidance for makers.
What the Current Site Conflates

The current brand guide tries to do too many jobs at once. Brand identity content is tangled with product design, engineering specs, and content strategy. The result is a document that architects love but designers and marketers struggle to use.

Brand identityPositioning, visual language, expression rules
Product mockupsFull app pages for Jobs, Salary, Profile, DLS site
Content routingBrand boundary map, data-attribute flows
Engineering specsCSS feTurbulence params, easing functions, tokens
UX flowsArchetype assessment, onboarding sequences

The fix is not to delete content. It is to separate brand identity (the guide) from product design (a different document). A brand guide should be usable by someone who will never write a line of CSS.

Section 02 — The Proposal

Five Pages,
Clear Lanes

The restructured guide follows a narrative arc: who we are, what we look like, how we apply it, how each expression works, and how archetypes personalize the experience.

01
Keep & Sharpen
Brand
The conceptual foundation. Currently the strongest page, but missing key pieces: a clear positioning statement, voice and tone guidance, and the brand story as a narrative rather than a system diagram.
  • Positioning statement and brand story
  • Voice and tone guidance with per-expression examples
  • Core principles (the "two brands, one system" thesis)
  • Brand architecture diagram (simplified)
02
Replaces “Foundations”
Visual Identity
Everything visual, organized by element rather than by technical implementation layer. A designer should be able to find "what colors do I use?" without understanding tokens and modes.
  • Color — 6 worlds, dark/light palettes, usage rules
  • Typography — DM Sans, Instrument Serif, JetBrains Mono hierarchy
  • Texture & Imagery — grain, duotone, halftone, Bauhaus blocks, photography direction
  • Spacing & Layout — grid system, card anatomy, surface rules
03
Replaces “Components”
Digital Application
Not a component library. A style guide for digital surfaces: how the visual identity comes to life on actual screens. Focused on patterns and principles, not React props or CSS classes.
  • Surface Modes in Practice — dark platform vs. light conference, when to use each
  • Interactive Patterns — hover states, transitions, micro-interactions (as visual guidance)
  • Cards & Content Units — speaker cards, job cards, article cards (anatomy + rules)
  • Assets & Templates — social graphics, OG images, email headers, print materials
04
Keep & Make Parallel
Expression Playbooks
Every expression gets the same structure, making it easy to compare and apply. No more hunting through different page layouts to find equivalent information. Merge Meetups into Community.
  • Each expression follows identical sections: Identity, Visual Example, Do/Don't, Key Assets
  • DLS (purple) — the conference brand, Bauhaus maximalism
  • Salary (neon green), Jobs (blue), Community (coral), Stack (crimson)
  • Season '26 (multi) — annual thematic expression
05
Radically Simplify
Archetypes
Keep the five personality archetypes as a powerful personalization layer. Cut everything that belongs in a product spec: UX flows, assessment logic, content routing, and boundary maps.
  • Five archetype cards: Ally (gold), Architect (teal), Pathfinder (blue), Anchor (coral), Cultivator (olive)
  • Personality descriptions and visual rules for each
  • Color palettes and gradient treatments
  • Usage guidelines: when and where archetypes appear

Parallel structure is the key. Every expression playbook should feel interchangeable in layout. If someone learns how the DLS page works, they already know how to navigate Jobs or Community. Same sections, same order, same depth.

Section 03 — Subtraction

What to Cut

These items are not bad work. They are product design and engineering artifacts that belong in separate documents: a product spec, a front-end style guide, or an internal wiki. They dilute the brand guide.

Full App Mockups
−
Jobs pages — full job listing, job detail, search results UI. These are product screens, not brand identity.
−
Salary pages — salary explorer, comparison tools, data visualizations. Belongs in product design specs.
−
Profile pages — user profiles, settings, onboarding flows. UX territory, not brand territory.
Full DLS Website Mockups
−
DLS homepage layout — a full page mockup is a wireframe exercise, not a brand guide artifact.
−
Speakers page — full speaker grid, filtering, detail views. Keep the speaker card design; cut the page layout.
−
Schedule and talks archive — functional UI pages that belong in a product spec.
System Architecture Artifacts
−
Content routing flows — diagrams showing how content moves between expressions. Internal engineering concern.
−
Brand boundary map — the decision tree for which brand applies where. Useful, but belongs in a separate operations doc.
−
Archetype UX flows — assessment sequences, onboarding steps, quiz logic. Product design, not brand identity.
Technical Implementation Details
−
CSS feTurbulence parameters — specific SVG filter values. Belongs in a front-end style guide or codebase docs.
−
Easing functions and transition specs — cubic-bezier values, animation durations. Engineering reference material.
−
Token names and data-attributes — var(--color-world-dls), data-archetype="ally". Internal API, not brand guidance.

Where does it go? None of this work is wasted. Product mockups move to Figma files. Technical specs move to the codebase README or a developer-facing style guide. Content routing moves to an internal operations wiki. The brand guide stays focused on brand.

Section 04 — Addition

What to Add

The current guide is strong on visual systems but weak on brand foundations. These additions fill the gap between "here's what it looks like" and "here's why it looks that way."

Brand Foundations
+
Brand positioning and story — a concise narrative: what DesignX is, who it serves, why it exists, and how it differs from alternatives. The anchor for every design decision.
+
Voice and tone guidance — how DesignX sounds in writing. Formal vs. casual spectrum, vocabulary preferences, writing examples for each expression. A copywriter should be able to use this page alone.
Visual Direction
+
Photography and illustration direction — what kind of images to commission or select. Subject matter, mood, cropping rules, color treatment. Do/don't examples.
+
Logo usage guidelines — the DesignX and DLS logos with clear space, minimum size, acceptable and unacceptable variations, placement rules on dark and light backgrounds.
Practical Tools
+
One-page cheat sheet — a single printable page with colors, type hierarchy, key dos and don'ts, and the expression color worlds. The thing someone tapes to their monitor.
+
Writing examples per expression — "Here's how DLS sounds. Here's how Salary sounds." Concrete copy samples, not abstract tone descriptors.

The positioning statement is the single most important addition. Every visual decision in the system flows from brand positioning. Without it, the guide is a collection of aesthetic choices without a thesis.

Voice and tone fills the biggest gap. The current guide can tell you what hex color to use for Community, but not what a Community blog post should sound like. That makes it half a brand guide.

Section 05 — The Philosophy

The Mindset
Shift

The restructuring is not just organizational. It requires a change in perspective: from documenting how the system is built to guiding what someone should do when they are making something.

Current: Architecture
"Here's how the system works."
  • Tokens, modes, and data-attributes
  • Implementation-first organization
  • Full-page product mockups
  • Audience: engineers and system architects
  • Answers: "What did we build?"
Proposed: Application
"Here's what to do when you're making something."
  • Decisions, principles, and examples
  • Task-first organization
  • Card-level patterns, not page-level mockups
  • Audience: designers, marketers, content creators
  • Answers: "What should I do?"
The Litmus Test
"I'm designing a social post for DLS — what do I do?"
Current guideNavigate tokens, find DLS mode, cross-reference Bauhaus block specs, check boundary rules
Restructured guideOpen Expression Playbooks → DLS → Key Assets → social post template and rules
"What colors should I use for a Community email header?"
Current guideFind color worlds section, locate Community, decode hex values, check dark/light rules
Restructured guideOpen Expression Playbooks → Community → Visual Example → coral palette with do/don't

The goal is not to dumb it down. The system architecture still exists, and engineers still need it. But it moves to a separate, technical reference document. The brand guide becomes what a brand guide is supposed to be: a tool for people who make things with the brand.
